How are coding agents changing how we study the economy, society and politics?

New results from a baseline survey with Tom Lyttelton and Maxim Massenkoff, about who is using AI coding agents and what they're doing with them. https://t.co/O10kvAVujT

If you're a quant social scientist with a pulse, you or your spam filter got a survey invite a couple months ago.

Then you deleted it.

Then you got a follow-up in which I begged you to click through.

Then 1,260 of you took pity and graciously participated. THANK YOU!

What did we find from your generous responses?

Even among a sample of Claude Code-curious researchers from March, only around a fifth of them have integrated coding agents into their regular workflow. https://t.co/pkEj6mr9Pb

But economists are 2x that; sociologists are half that.

Adoption is concentrated among early career researchers and researchers at high status universities. Men are adopting at twice the rate of women. https://t.co/z3srX9P9e7

Researchers are mostly *not* using AI to draft and ideate; mainly it's coding and editing work.

(I guess I'm the only one who admits to having a paper factory:)
https://t.co/7JiVi1EKHm https://t.co/AYOZm8X6pC

In a descriptive comparison between coding agent users and other researchers in the same discipline and career stage, the coding agent users are starting more projects, submitting more grants and posting more working papers. https://t.co/TlcqvX3OPB

BUT, coding agent users are not submitting more to journals. This might be a timing issue: projects take time to develop and we asked respondents (in March) about output over the preceding 6 months.  Many had likely only been using agents for a month or two.

Or it might reflect that you can speedrun through a first draft of a paper using agents.

But then getting it up to the level of journal submission requires the author catching up on a bunch of decisions and technical debt.
