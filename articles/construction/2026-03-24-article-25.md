been talking to vertical ai SaaS founders and seeing this pattern over and over:

founders trying to reverse engineer success by studying which verticals are “hot” right now

they see some company raised a series A for AI in construction or AI for restaurants or whatever

they think “i can build that, the market is huge, theres room for multiple winners”

so they build a product that sounds great in pitch decks

“we use AI to automate scheduling and inventory management for restaurant chains, addressing a 50B market”

clean pitch, good market size, hits all the VC buzzwords

then they go to market and discover:

the restaurant owners dont want “AI scheduling”. they want the thing that solves the specific problem where their weekend manager keeps scheduling too many servers for sunday brunch and not enough for saturday dinner, and they want it to account for the fact that saturdays are different if theres a home game

the product the founder built does generic scheduling. it doesnt know about home games. it doesnt know that this restaurant has a bunch of regulars who only come on thursdays. it doesnt have the 15 small features that would make someone switch

meanwhile theres some person who had a restaurant family background for 8 years, hated the existing tools, started building their own thing nights and weekends

their product is ugly. the ui looks like it was designed in 2015. but it has every small feature that matters. when restaurant managers see it they say “holy shit this person actually understands my job”

the founder with the pretty pitch deck is still trying to figure out product market fit

the former restaurant manager has 50 paying customers and is growing 15% month over month

can’t recommend becoming an expert in a vertical as fast as possible

if you’ve raised money, buy that deep expertise as fast as possible
