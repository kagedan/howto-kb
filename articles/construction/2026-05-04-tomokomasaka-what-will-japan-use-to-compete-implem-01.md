[What will Japan use to compete? – Implementation capabilities honed by the necessity of "doing it or the site won't function"]

The US has an advantage through the power of its platforms. China seizes market share with its scale and speed. So what weapon will Japan use?

The Japanese construction industry is facing an aging workforce and a serious labor shortage. The 2024 problem (the application of overtime regulations to the construction industry) has also compounded the issue. AI construction machinery is being introduced not because "we want to," but because "we have to do it or the site won't function." This urgent necessity has forged a unique implementation capability, even on a global scale.

Kajima Corporation's A4CSEL uses AI to coordinate four types of construction machinery, automating a series of embankment operations (loading → transporting → leveling → compaction). It enables remote management of multiple automated construction machines by a single controller in an office away from the site. In January 2026, its application to highway construction was announced, expanding its scope beyond dam construction. Obayashi Corporation is using AI to automate blasting technology in mountain tunnels, and Shimizu Corporation is autonomously operating Smart Dozer on fluctuating terrain.

All of this is knowledge accumulated from actual construction sites where weather and ground conditions change and unexpected situations occur on a daily basis. There is a fundamental difference between simulation results and practical knowledge gained from getting your hands dirty.

The question is how to scale this strength. Making the tacit knowledge accumulated by each general contractor at their own sites shareable through an open platform like Opera, connecting it to international standards, and spreading it through the network of allied countries—this is likely the most realistic path for Japan.
